Animated eye blink GIF

For my first animated GIF, I made a blinking animation using artwork I created in Adobe Illustrator and then brought into Photoshop. I separated the eye into different layers so I could control each eyelid of the movement more easily. In Photoshop, I used the Timeline panel to build a frame-by-frame animation, adjusting the eyelid slightly in each frame to show the eye opening and closing. I kept the motion simple so the blink would loop smoothly and be easy to follow. I also played around with the frame delays to get the speed to feel more natural. This project helped me see how even small changes between frames can make something feel alive and expressive.

Animated rose moving GIF

For my second animated GIF, I created a cut-up cinema animation using a photograph and Photoshop’s Puppet Warp tool. I began by selecting and isolating the figure from the background. I chose to use the illustrated environment I previously created because I thought it would be visually engaging to have a flower moving within that setting. After placing pins on the rose to control its movement, I duplicated the image multiple times and made subtle adjustments to the flower in each layer. These small changes helped create a sense of motion throughout the sequence. I then arranged each pose as a separate frame in the Timeline panel and experimented with different playback speeds until the animation appeared smooth and fluid. I also considered how gesture, repetition, and rhythm could enhance the effectiveness of a short looping animation. This process demonstrated how GIFs can transform a still image fun and expressive moving composition.
